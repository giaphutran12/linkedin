# LinkedIn Content Strategy Brief

Date: 2026-03-23

## Working correction

Edward is not a founder, VC, recruiter, or seasoned operator posting "from above."

He is a young SWE with real startup experience, real shipped work, and unusually strong builder receipts for his age.

The job of his content is not to cosplay seniority.

The job is to make the right people think:

- founders: this guy is cracked, i should hire or talk to him
- recruiters / hiring managers: this candidate is unusually strong for startup, mid-size, or big tech roles
- VCs / ecosystem people: this builder is worth noticing and could become investable
- fellow SWEs: this person ships real work and is worth connecting with

## What changed from the bad framing

The wrong framing would have pushed Edward toward "founder content" or generic "thought leadership."

That is a trap.

For a 22-year-old SWE, the highest-credibility position is:

1. builder with receipts
2. sharp observer of startup execution
3. honest narrator of what he is learning while shipping
4. young engineer who can already think in product, speed, tradeoffs, and business impact

That means his strongest posts should sound like:

- "here's what i built"
- "here's what broke"
- "here's what i learned from shipping under pressure"
- "here's the exact result"
- "here's the part nobody tells junior engineers"

Not:

- "founders should..."
- "VCs need to..."
- "how to scale orgs"
- "leadership lessons" without lived proof

## Changes versus prior draft

Compared with the prior founder-leaning draft, this version makes four explicit corrections:

1. audience fit

The old posture implied Edward should sound like a founder / operator speaking downward.

This version repositions him as a young SWE speaking from lived builder experience to audiences above, beside, and around him.

2. success metric

The old posture risked optimizing for broad engagement and generic "personal brand" growth.

This version distinguishes vanity reach from actual hireability, credibility, and founder / recruiter interest.

3. content selection

The old posture risked over-indexing on founder takes, startup commentary, and abstract thought leadership.

This version prioritizes proof-of-work, demos, technical tradeoffs, business impact, and concrete artifacts.

4. audience nuance

The old posture risked treating founders, recruiters, VCs, and SWEs as one generic LinkedIn audience.

This version separates what each audience actually notices and what kind of post is most persuasive to each.

## Highest-signal conclusions

### 1. Breakout reach and hireability are not the same thing

The content most likely to spike impressions is usually:

- emotionally resonant
- highly relatable
- easy to consume in seconds
- broad enough for non-engineers to react to

The content most likely to get Edward hired is usually:

- specific
- evidence-heavy
- attached to a real build, demo, repo, metric, or technical tradeoff
- legible to people who hire engineers

Best strategy: do both, but do not confuse them.

### 2. The winning position is "young engineer with abnormal proof of work"

Founders hiring early engineers consistently respond to:

- side projects
- generalist range
- speed
- willingness to build through ambiguity
- proof that someone hacks on real things outside class or job requirements

Source-backed examples:

- Alan Wang's founding engineer post explicitly says Serra wants someone who has "hacked together cool projects" and says recent graduates are welcome: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alanhlwang_were-hiring-for-our-first-founding-engineer-activity-7258138236873539586-uYc7>
- Irad Cohen's post says first engineers should be generalists who can "make anything happen," not narrow unicorn-stack hires: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/iradcn_hiring-your-startups-first-engineer-lessons-activity-7250092496372776960--SPq>
- Guillermo Flor's referral post for a founding engineer says "new grad," "project-driven," and "always building stuff relentlessly": <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/guillermoflor_novoflow-is-looking-for-founding-engineer-activity-7369461185244491778-Lt3l>

Inference: Edward should bias hard toward posts that make him look relentlessly buildy, not broadly opinionated.

### 3. Receipts beat aesthetics

Edward's own local performance data already points this way:

- raw screenshots beat polished graphics
- demos beat abstract explanation
- personal hook + technical substance beats pure educational posting
- AI-generated visuals underperform because they look fake

This matches what LinkedIn itself recommends: create helpful, professional content that adds value and fosters meaningful conversation, while avoiding low-value promotion, engagement bait, and unoriginal content: <https://www.linkedin.com/blog/content-recommendations-avoid>

Inference: if Edward can show the terminal, the product, the latency, the cost drop, the bug, or the weird edge case, he should.

### 4. Comments and conversations matter more than applause

Official LinkedIn creator resources emphasize expertise, real opportunities, and audience engagement: <https://members.linkedin.com/linkedin-creators-resources/content-best-practices>

LinkedIn's content recommendations page also explicitly favors content that is helpful and professional and encourages meaningful conversations: <https://www.linkedin.com/blog/content-recommendations-avoid>

Inference: the metric to care about is not raw likes. It is whether the post causes:

- thoughtful comments
- profile clicks
- recruiter / founder DMs
- warm intros
- "send me your resume / portfolio / GitHub"

## Breakout vs. consistent-performance traits

### Breakout / viral traits

These are more likely to create spikes in impressions:

| Trait | Why it spreads | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| strong emotional hook | broad audience can react fast | may be memorable but not hireable |
| identity / frustration / contrarian take | easy to comment on | can become pure discourse bait |
| high-relatability story | works outside engineering niche | weak proof of technical strength |
| surprising demo or visual | stops scroll immediately | if shallow, becomes novelty only |
| "i can't believe this worked" energy | curiosity + entertainment | can tilt into hype |

Examples:

- Edward's local dengue / LinkedIn fraud post and "i cannot believe" style posts performed better than some technical posts because they had emotional hooks
- Brett Bouchard's post about building a voice assistant for his dad works because it is deeply personal and instantly legible, while still being a real build: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/brettbouchard_a-personal-medical-history-ai-voice-assistant-activity-7340756109147717634-K7nx>
- Toni Lopez's Karumi launch post combines founder struggle, strong momentum, and an instant demo: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/tonilopezmr_were-launching-karumi-today-on-our-first-activity-7393762204820586496-0xM3>

### Consistent-performance traits

These are more likely to compound reputation with the right audience over time:

| Trait | Why it compounds | Best audience |
|---|---|---|
| clear proof of shipping | establishes competence | founders, recruiters, SWEs |
| quantified outcomes | shows business awareness | founders, recruiters, VCs |
| repeatable content lanes | makes Edward legible | all four audiences |
| technical specificity without jargon soup | signals depth and clarity | SWEs, founders, hiring managers |
| honest tradeoff writing | signals judgment | founders, senior engineers |
| public demos / repos / artifacts | lowers skepticism | recruiters, founders, SWEs |

Examples:

- Alan Wang's hiring post grounds the role in revenue, customer count, and company stage, not vague hype: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alanhlwang_were-hiring-for-our-first-founding-engineer-activity-7258138236873539586-uYc7>
- Kyle Richards says engineering CVs stand out when they show impact, achievements, and relevant side projects, not stack laundry lists: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kylerichardskioni_softwareengineering-careers-hiring-activity-7260202649252249602-wgD2>
- Pasquale Bruno argues GitHub alone is not enough; work needs context, visuals, methodology, and business impact: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/pasqualeclaudiobruno_just-put-your-projects-on-github-recruiters-activity-7361003526010875904-L6pY>

## Vanity engagement vs. real credibility signal

### Vanity engagement

This can look good but often does not convert into respect or opportunity:

- broad motivational takes
- generic AI hot takes
- recycled startup platitudes
- engagement-bait questions
- "5 lessons every founder should know" from someone who has not lived it
- surface-level tech explainers with no artifact

LinkedIn explicitly warns against engagement bait, low-value promotion, and unoriginal posting: <https://www.linkedin.com/blog/content-recommendations-avoid>

### Real credibility / hireability signal

This is what is more likely to make the right people reach out:

- shipping a real product or feature
- showing before/after business impact
- writing clearly about constraints and tradeoffs
- attaching a demo, repo, screenshot, benchmark, or rollout artifact
- proving ownership, not just participation
- showing pattern recognition from repeated execution

High-signal post ingredients for Edward:

1. a real problem
2. a real build
3. one technical challenge that makes the work non-trivial
4. one business/user result
5. one artifact: screenshot, screen recording, repo, benchmark, or concrete output

## Audience differences

Edward should not treat "LinkedIn audience" as one blob.

### Founders

What they care about:

- can this person ship
- can this person handle ambiguity
- can this person think beyond code
- can this person move fast with limited hand-holding

Signals that work:

- "built X because Y was broken"
- speed, scrappiness, working demos
- metrics tied to user or revenue outcomes
- evidence of side projects and obsession

Sources:

- Alan Wang on wanting someone who has hacked together cool projects: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alanhlwang_were-hiring-for-our-first-founding-engineer-activity-7258138236873539586-uYc7>
- Irad Cohen on hiring a generalist octopus, not a stack unicorn: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/iradcn_hiring-your-startups-first-engineer-lessons-activity-7250092496372776960--SPq>
- Tim He on founding engineer hiring being a trust-heavy bet, not a normal hire: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/timhe2000_hiring-a-founding-engineer-sucks-because-activity-7356744676554559490-u0Kd>

### Recruiters / hiring managers

What they care about:

- clear evidence of competence
- clean narrative
- measurable impact
- legible projects
- easy-to-evaluate proof

Signals that work:

- metrics in plain English
- clear "what i built / why it mattered"
- strong GitHub / demo hygiene
- not too many half-baked projects

Sources:

- Kyle Richards on impact, achievements, and personal projects mattering more than stack lists: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kylerichardskioni_softwareengineering-careers-hiring-activity-7260202649252249602-wgD2>
- Pasquale Bruno on recruiters needing a "shop window" not a pile of repos: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/pasqualeclaudiobruno_just-put-your-projects-on-github-recruiters-activity-7361003526010875904-L6pY>
- Askruit on the market becoming a "war for precision" where digital presence decides top-10 shortlist status: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/askruit_myth-we-cant-find-qualified-candidates-activity-7410956943235710976-t2bD>

### VCs / ecosystem people

What they care about:

- is this person unusually high-agency
- do they build things people actually want
- do they understand product, speed, and leverage
- could this person become a founder or early technical hire worth backing

Signals that work:

- prototypes with obvious real-world use
- proof of taste and product instinct
- ability to turn technical work into a clear story
- visible momentum and seriousness

Sources:

- Brian Hecht says investors should expect a working demo in early meetings because it signals builders who get things done: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/brianhecht_for-25-years-as-a-non-technical-founder-activity-7349156469776269314-1onZ>
- YC launch-style posts like Toni Lopez's work because the demo and product legibility are immediate: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/tonilopezmr_were-launching-karumi-today-on-our-first-activity-7393762204820586496-0xM3>

Inference: VCs are not the primary audience, but they do notice technically strong young people who demonstrate agency, product instinct, and speed.

### Fellow SWEs

What they care about:

- is the work real
- is the technical problem interesting
- did this person actually learn something non-obvious
- do they sound like an engineer, not a content marketer

Signals that work:

- terminal screenshots
- architecture simplifications
- bug stories
- cost / latency / reliability tradeoffs
- "here's exactly what broke and how i fixed it"

This is where Edward's current best technical content already points.

Angle:

"not just what i built, but what changed because it shipped"

### Lane 3. AI engineering without fake guru energy

Angle:

"here's what actually happens when you try to make AI features real"

Best topics:

- orchestration pain
- evals
- latency / cost tradeoffs
- prompt iteration
- where the real work is not the model call

This position matches founder and SWE interest while staying honest to Edward's level.

### Lane 4. demos of weird or useful side projects

Angle:

"i built this because i was annoyed by a real problem"

This lane has the best chance of crossing from credibility into breakout reach, especially if the artifact is visually obvious.

Best pattern:

1. relatable annoyance
2. why existing solutions were bad
3. quick demo
4. one technical twist
5. invite builders to comment

### Lane 5. startup engineer reality

Angle:

"software engineering is not sexy 80% of the time, but this is what makes you dangerous"

This lane should stay grounded in lived experience:

- debugging
- boring reliability work
- working around bad APIs
- cleaning edge cases
- building unglamorous internal tools

Done well, this makes founders trust him more because it signals he is not addicted to shiny demos only.

### Lane 6. honest growth from student -> serious builder

Angle:

"here's how i'm leveling up in public"

This is not generic self-improvement content.

It should be anchored to:

- what changed in how he builds
- what he used to get wrong
- what startup work taught him that school did not

This lane is strong for recruiters and peers if it stays concrete.

## What Edward should avoid

### Do not borrow authority from roles he does not hold

Avoid:

- generic founder advice
- investor commentary without direct exposure
- recruiter advice as if he is in hiring
- management content

He can still speak to those audiences.

He just has to speak from his real seat:

- "as a young SWE, here's what i notice"
- "working at a startup taught me this"
- "here's what makes me want to join a team"
- "here's what i learned shipping under pressure"

### Do not chase broad impressions at the expense of legibility

A post can do numbers and still fail the actual goal if nobody leaves with:

- what Edward builds
- how strong he is
- what kinds of roles he fits

### Do not make GitHub the whole pitch

Public GitHub helps only when the work is legible.

Pasquale Bruno's "shop window" framing is useful here: context and packaging matter as much as the raw repo link: <https://www.linkedin.com/posts/pasqualeclaudiobruno_just-put-your-projects-on-github-recruiters-activity-7361003526010875904-L6pY>

## Practical posting principles

## Decision filter for future post ideas

Before publishing, ask:

1. does this make Edward sound like a builder, not a pundit
2. is there a real receipt here: metric, demo, screenshot, repo, or hard-earned lesson
3. would a founder or recruiter leave with a clearer sense of why Edward is unusually strong
4. is he speaking from his real seat as a young SWE, not borrowing authority from a role he does not hold

If the answer to two or more of these is "no," the idea likely needs to be reframed or dropped.

### 1. one post = one proof

Each post should make one thing easier to believe:

- Edward ships
- Edward thinks clearly
- Edward learns fast
- Edward cares about useful products
- Edward is worth talking to

### 2. say what changed

This is a better pattern than listing tools.

Bad:

"used Next.js, Tailwind, Postgres, OpenAI, Redis"

Better:

"cut analysis cost by 70%"

### 3. make the artifact unavoidable

If there is a:

- screenshot
- recording
- benchmark
- repo
- dashboard
- ugly sketch

include it.

### 4. write for scanability, not literary elegance

This matches both LinkedIn norms and Edward's own voice guide.

### 5. invite the right type of response

Better CTAs:

- founders: "if you're hiring engineers who like messy problems, let's talk"
- SWEs: "curious how others would have handled this edge case"
- recruiters / hiring managers: "happy to share the demo / repo"

Avoid empty engagement bait.
